# Team Expertise

## Overview

This project requires expertise spanning microsimulation modeling, statistical methods, survey data calibration, Social Security policy, and software engineering. The proposed team leadership brings together complementary skills in these areas through a collaboration between PolicyEngine and external experts in statistics and retirement economics.

## Proposed Team Leadership

### Max Ghenis

Max Ghenis founded PolicyEngine in 2021 and serves as its President. He holds a Bachelor's degree in Operations Research and Management Science from UC Berkeley (2008) and a Master's degree in Data, Economics, and Development Policy from MIT (2020). Before founding PolicyEngine, he worked as a data scientist at Google from 2010 to 2018, where he co-founded the People Analytics Data Science team. He also founded and led the UBI Center, a nonprofit think tank focused on universal basic income research, from 2019 to 2023.

At PolicyEngine, Ghenis has led development of microsimulation models for multiple countries and created the infrastructure for the Enhanced CPS (ECPS). The ECPS development involved designing and implementing the two-stage methodology combining quantile regression forest imputation with gradient descent calibration to over 7,000 administrative targets. This work demonstrated that publicly available synthetic data can achieve accuracy comparable to restricted administrative data for tax-benefit microsimulation.

For this project, Ghenis would provide technical leadership on software architecture and implementation, integration with the PolicyEngine ecosystem, data infrastructure development including adaptation of microimpute and microcalibrate tools, and overall project management and coordination.

### Ben Ogorek

Ben Ogorek earned both his Bachelor's and PhD degrees in Statistics from North Carolina State University and has worked as a data scientist since 2008. His research background includes work on regression methods, multiple testing, and information sharing, with published research in Statistical Applications of Genetics and Molecular Biology. He has contributed to PolicyEngine projects and brings expertise in statistical modeling and machine learning methods.

For this project, Ogorek would contribute expertise in quantile regression forests and distributional imputation methods, statistical validation frameworks and diagnostic testing, hazard models for demographic transitions, and methodology for assessing model performance and uncertainty quantification.

### John Sabelhaus

John Sabelhaus is a Senior Fellow at the Wisconsin Center for Financial Security. He previously served in senior research positions at the Federal Reserve Board and the Congressional Budget Office, where he was Chief of Long Term Modeling and oversaw development of integrated micro/macro models of Social Security and Medicare. His research focuses on retirement economics, household finance, and Social Security policy.

For this project, Sabelhaus would provide guidance on Social Security benefit rules and policy interpretation, validation of model assumptions against economic theory and empirical patterns, specification of appropriate calibration targets from SSA administrative data, and connections to the academic literature and policy community.

## Collaborative Approach

The project would leverage PolicyEngine's existing infrastructure and Ghenis's software engineering expertise, combine this with Ogorek's statistical methodology skills for imputation and validation, and integrate Sabelhaus's domain expertise in Social Security and retirement economics. This division of responsibilities mirrors the successful collaboration model used in developing the ECPS, where technical infrastructure, statistical methods, and domain expertise all proved essential.

Regular coordination through weekly team meetings, code review processes, and collaborative documentation would ensure that statistical rigor, policy accuracy, and implementation quality all meet high standards. The open-source nature of the project allows for transparency in methods and reproducibility of results, with all team members committed to public documentation and academic-quality validation.

## Track Record

PolicyEngine has successfully developed microsimulation models for six countries (US, UK, Canada, Israel, Nigeria, Australia), implementing hundreds of state and federal programs with thousands of policy parameters. The ECPS development demonstrated the team's capability to execute complex data construction projects involving machine learning imputation and large-scale calibration. Ogorek's statistical research background and Sabelhaus's extensive Social Security policy expertise complement the existing PolicyEngine infrastructure and development capabilities.
